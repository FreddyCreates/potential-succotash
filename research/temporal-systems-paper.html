Temporal Consensus in Living Systems: Epoch Management and Evolution Protocols

On epoch transitions, version coexistence, sunset clauses, and evolution control for adaptive AI systems

I. Abstract

Adaptive intelligence systems accumulate state, responsibilities, and historical commitments. This paper presents temporal consensus as the governance discipline that coordinates model evolution over time. It introduces epoch management, migration windows, sunset clauses, and replay-based reconciliation as the foundation for lawful system change.

II. Introduction

Most failure in long-running AI systems comes from poorly coordinated change rather than from isolated bugs. Versions overlap, assumptions drift, and obsolete powers remain active long after their legitimacy has expired.

Temporal consensus solves this by turning time into a first-class governance primitive.

III. Epoch Management

An epoch is a bounded governance interval with its own rules, active models, and admissible interfaces. Epoch boundaries are published in advance and tied to measurable readiness conditions.

This makes change visible to both machines and humans. Every node can reason about the same activation frontier.

IV. Version Transition Protocols

Version transitions should not be binary when the cost of abrupt replacement is high. A migration window allows old and new components to operate side by side while compatibility evidence is gathered.

During coexistence, policy engines verify that outputs remain semantically aligned and that rollback remains possible.

Compatibility = SchemaMatch × BehaviorMatch × AuditTrace

V. Sunset Clauses

A sunset clause is a constitutional timer on power. It guarantees that temporary privileges, deprecated interfaces, and emergency measures expire unless expressly renewed.

This prevents governance sediment from hardening into permanent technical debt.

  • Emergency powers expire first unless renewed by elevated quorum.
  • Legacy interfaces persist only through the declared migration window.
  • Expired capabilities remain visible in logs but inactive in runtime policy.

VI. Temporal Consensus

Temporal consensus requires more than synchronized clocks. It requires agreement on ordering, replay semantics, and the canonical source of epoch truth.

When messages arrive out of order, replay and reconciliation re-establish a common constitutional timeline.

CanonicalOrder = hash(chain, timestamp, epoch_marker)

VII. Evolution in Living AI Systems

Living systems evolve by preserving identity while changing implementation. For AI, that means updating models and policies without abandoning obligations to users, auditors, or federated peers.

Temporal governance ensures that learning remains civilizationally coherent rather than merely adaptive.

VIII. Conclusion

Temporal consensus gives evolving AI systems a lawful method for change. It converts upgrades, deprecations, and rollbacks into charter-governed events, reducing fragility while preserving institutional memory.

Canonical use: this paper explains why epoch declarations, migration windows, and sunset clauses are treated as enforceable governance mechanisms rather than release-management preferences.
