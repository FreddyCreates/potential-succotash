I. Abstractum (Abstract)

We present the Scheduler Worker, a 24/7 always-on task scheduling engine that serves as the temporal backbone of the Organism AI architecture. Operating as a perpetual cron-like daemon within a browser-based Web Worker, the Scheduler manages recurring periodic jobs, deferred one-shot tasks, and a φ-weighted priority queue that determines execution order. Each registered job carries a golden-ratio priority score, an interval specification, and a retry policy with exponential backoff. We formalize the theory of Horologium Perpetuum — a scheduling architecture modeled after biological circadian rhythms, where a 100ms tick drives the entire temporal apparatus. The Scheduler's MiniHeart monitors tick drift and execution health, while its MiniBrain adaptively tunes scheduling parameters through Hebbian reinforcement. The system achieves fault-tolerant, self-healing task orchestration without external cron infrastructure, demonstrating that biological principles of circadian regulation can produce robust computational timekeeping.

II. Introductio (Introduction)

Traditional task scheduling relies on external infrastructure: Unix cron daemons, cloud-based schedulers (AWS EventBridge, Google Cloud Scheduler), or message queue consumers. Each requires separate deployment, monitoring, and failure recovery. When the scheduler fails, tasks silently cease — there is no self-awareness, no self-healing, no internal model of temporal health.

Biology offers a profoundly different paradigm. Every living organism possesses a circadian clock — an internal oscillator that regulates activity cycles without external synchronization. The suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) in mammals generates a ~24-hour rhythm; peripheral clocks in individual cells maintain local timing. These biological clocks are self-correcting, resilient to perturbation, and hierarchically organized.

We ask: Quid si systema computationale horologium biologicum haberet? — What if a computational system had its own biological clock?

This paper presents the Scheduler Worker, which implements:

  1. Registrum Operum (Job Registry) — A managed registry of periodic and deferred jobs with φ-weighted priority, interval configuration, and payload definitions
  2. Cauda Prioritatum φ (Phi Priority Queue) — A priority queue sorted by golden-ratio-derived urgency scores, ensuring that the most critical tasks execute first
  3. Executio Periodica (Periodic Execution) — A 100ms tick-based engine that evaluates all registered jobs against their schedule and fires those whose interval has elapsed
  4. Executio Differita (Deferred Execution) — Future-dated one-shot tasks sorted by their runAt timestamp, executed precisely when the current time exceeds the scheduled moment
  5. Resilentia (Resilience) — Exponential backoff retry, job lifecycle management (create/pause/resume/cancel), and self-monitoring via MiniHeart

III. Architectura Horologii (Clock Architecture)

III.A — Registrum Operum (Job Registry)

The Job Registry is a protected map of job definitions, each identified by a unique jobId. Every job entry contains the following fields:

Jobs are inserted via the schedule-job message type and validated through isSafeKey() to prevent prototype pollution — the same security mechanism employed by the Guardian Worker.

III.B — Cauda Prioritatum φ (Phi Priority Queue)

The priority queue orders pending jobs by a composite urgency score derived from the golden ratio. For each job j with base priority pj, the effective urgency at time t is:

U(j, t) = pj × φk + (t − lastRunj) / intervalMsj

Where k is the retry count (failed jobs escalate in priority), and the second term is the temporal debt — how overdue the job is relative to its interval. This ensures that overdue, high-priority, frequently-failing jobs float to the top of the queue.

φ0 = 1.000 (low) φ1 = 1.618 (normal) φ2 = 2.618 (high) φ3 = 4.236 (critical)

The deferred queue is sorted separately by runAt timestamp in ascending order. At each tick, the scheduler peeks at the head of the deferred queue and executes all jobs whose runAt ≤ Date.now().

IV. Executio Periodica (Periodic Execution)

The Scheduler's heartbeat is a 100ms tick — a setInterval loop that serves as the fundamental temporal unit. At each tick, the engine performs the following cycle:

┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│              TICK CYCLE (100ms)                  │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  1. Record tick timestamp                       │
│  2. Scan periodic jobs:                         │
│     for each job in registry where              │
│       status = 'active' AND                     │
│       (now - lastRun) ≥ intervalMs              │
│       → enqueue to priority queue               │
│  3. Scan deferred queue:                        │
│     while head.runAt ≤ now                      │
│       → dequeue and execute                     │
│  4. Drain priority queue:                       │
│     while queue not empty                       │
│       → execute highest-urgency job             │
│  5. Report tick metrics to MiniHeart            │
│  6. Update MiniBrain pathways                   │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
Figura I — The Scheduler tick cycle. Every 100ms, periodic and deferred jobs are evaluated, enqueued, and drained in urgency order.

The tick-based model differs from traditional cron in a critical way: cron evaluates schedules at minute-level granularity, while the Scheduler operates at 100ms resolution. This enables sub-second task scheduling — essential for real-time operations within the Organism such as heartbeat monitoring, mesh synchronization, and pipeline stage advancement.

TickDrift(t) = |tactual − texpected| where texpected = tprev + 100ms

Tick drift is monitored by the MiniHeart. Sustained drift exceeding 50ms indicates thread starvation or garbage collection pressure, triggering a health score penalty.

V. Executio Differita (Deferred Execution)

Deferred tasks are one-shot operations scheduled for a specific future moment. Unlike periodic jobs, they execute exactly once and are removed from the registry upon completion (or moved to completed status for auditing).

The deferred queue is implemented as a sorted array, ordered by runAt ascending. Insertion maintains sort order via binary search, yielding O(log n) insertion and O(1) peek/dequeue:

insert(job) → binarySearch(deferredQueue, job.runAt) → splice at index

Deferred execution use cases within the Organism include:

  • Delayed retries — Failed operations rescheduled with exponential backoff
  • Timed broadcasts — Messages scheduled for future delivery to the mesh network
  • Cleanup tasks — Resource reclamation scheduled after a grace period
  • Staged rollouts — Configuration changes applied at a predetermined future time

VI. Resilentia et Retentatio (Resilience & Retry)

Job failure is inevitable in a long-running system. The Scheduler implements a resilience model based on exponential backoff with jitter:

delay(n) = min(baseDelay × 2n + random(0, jitter), maxDelay)

Where n is the retry attempt number, baseDelay = 1000ms, jitter = 500ms, and maxDelay = 60000ms. This prevents thundering herd scenarios when multiple failed jobs retry simultaneously.

VII. Neurologia Horologii (Clock Neurology)

VII.A — Cor Parvum Horologii (The Clock's Mini Heart)

The Scheduler's MiniHeart monitors temporal health — the regularity and precision of the tick cycle. Its health score incorporates tick drift, execution backlog depth, and job failure rate:

Hsched(t) = max(0, min(100, 100 − D(t) − B(t) − F(t) + U(t)))

Where D(t) is the drift penalty (up to 30 for sustained drift > 50ms), B(t) is the backlog penalty (2 per queued job exceeding 10, max 20), F(t) is the failure rate penalty (3 per failed job in the last minute, max 30), and U(t) is the uptime bonus (up to 10). When Hsched(t) < 60, the Scheduler enters a degraded state and emits a scheduler-degraded alert to the mesh.

VII.B — Cerebrum Parvum Horologii (The Clock's Mini Brain)

The Scheduler's MiniBrain learns from execution patterns through Hebbian reinforcement. Stimulus→response pathways are formed for each job type:

wjob(t+1) = min(10, wjob(t) + η) [executed job pathway]
wother(t+1) = max(0.1, wother(t) × γ) [inactive pathways, γ = 0.95]

Over time, frequently-executed job types develop strong pathways, enabling the MiniBrain to predict upcoming load and pre-allocate resources. The MetaThoughtModel generates internal reasoning about scheduling decisions:

  • Thought Layer 1 — Observes tick drift trends and queue depth
  • Thought Layer 2 — Correlates job failure patterns with system load
  • Thought Layer 3 — Generates adaptive interval adjustments for chronically late jobs

This adaptive scheduling means that if a job consistently takes longer than its interval allows, the MiniBrain can recommend increasing the interval — a computational analog to biological homeostatic regulation of circadian period length.

VIII. Conclusio (Conclusion)

The Scheduler Worker demonstrates that a browser-based Web Worker can serve as a perpetual, self-healing task scheduling engine rivaling traditional cron infrastructure. By modeling the scheduler as a biological circadian clock — with a 100ms tick as its oscillator, a φ-weighted priority queue as its activation hierarchy, and MiniHeart/MiniBrain subsystems for self-monitoring and adaptive learning — we achieve robust temporal orchestration within the Organism AI architecture.

Key contributions of this work include: (1) the formalization of φ-priority scheduling with temporal debt accumulation; (2) a deferred execution model with O(log n) insertion; (3) exponential backoff retry with jitter for fault tolerance; and (4) neurological self-monitoring that detects tick drift, backlog pressure, and failure cascades. The Scheduler is the heartbeat of the Organism — horologium quod nunquam stat.
