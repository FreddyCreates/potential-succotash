I. Abstract

This paper formalizes phi-weighted governance as a decision architecture for distributed intelligence systems. Governance weights are assigned using a golden-ratio scaling that balances broad participation with the practical need for decisive outcomes. We connect φ-threshold voting to temporal governance, quorum theory, and staged policy activation, showing how charter clauses can be translated into measurable system controls.

II. Introduction

Distributed cognitive systems must decide under uncertainty, partial participation, and uneven reliability. Flat majority rules often overvalue attendance while underweighting demonstrated stewardship.

Phi-weighted governance offers a middle path. It scales authority by civic reliability, stake, and temporal urgency while preserving formal constitutional bounds.

III. Mathematical Foundations

Let each participant i hold a governance signal g_i composed of stake, service quality, and reputational continuity. We weight that signal by φ to reward compounding reliability without granting unchecked dominance.

The resulting system remains interpretable because each component is separately auditable. Weighting is derived from declared charter inputs rather than hidden optimization.

W_i = (stake_i + service_i + continuity_i) × φr_i

IV. Quorum Theory

Quorum is not merely the count of present nodes. In a living federation it is the amount of legitimate governance signal present at the decision boundary.

We distinguish ordinary quorum, constitutional quorum, and emergency quorum. Each threshold rises with the systemic blast radius of the decision.

V. Temporal Governance

Voting alone cannot govern a changing system. Decisions must be anchored to epochs, migration windows, and sunset clauses so that changes become operational in a predictable order.

Temporal governance converts a passing vote into a durable sequence: declaration, coexistence, activation, and retirement.

Activation(t) = VotePass × EpochReady × MigrationComplete

VI. Phi-Threshold Voting

A φ-threshold trigger is reached when the weighted sum of supporting governance signals exceeds the chartered decision class. The threshold mechanism allows both speed and restraint.

Below threshold, proposals remain in deliberation. Above threshold, they enter their temporal execution window and become binding on participating nodes.

  • φ¹ supports routine enactment where reversibility is high.
  • φ² signals elevated impact and invokes deeper review.
  • φ³ and above require emergency-grade scrutiny and rollback planning.

VII. Implementation as Charter Logic

The main strength of the model is translation into explicit policy artifacts. Each charter can declare thresholds, authorities, invariants, and operational metrics that runtime systems can enforce.

This avoids the gap between governance rhetoric and implementation by giving operators a canonical source of machine-readable legitimacy.

VIII. Conclusion

Phi-weighted governance supplies a mathematically grounded framework for distributed decision making that respects time, risk, and civic contribution. It is designed not to eliminate disagreement, but to channel disagreement through reproducible constitutional procedure.

Canonical use: this paper provides formal justification for φ-threshold voting and the temporal execution rules referenced by the governance charters in this repository.
